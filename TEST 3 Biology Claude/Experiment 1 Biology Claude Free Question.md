
What is photosynthesis?

Photosynthesis is the process plants, algae, and some bacteria use to convert light energy into chemical energy stored as sugar. It's essentially how these organisms make their own food using sunlight.

Here's how it works at a basic level:

Plants take in carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the air through tiny pores in their leaves, absorb water (H₂O) through their roots, and capture sunlight using a green pigment called **chlorophyll**, which is found in cell structures called chloroplasts. Using the energy from light, they convert these raw materials into glucose (a sugar) and release oxygen as a byproduct.

The overall reaction can be summarized as:

**6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂**

In words: carbon dioxide plus water plus light produces glucose plus oxygen.

The process happens in two main stages:

The **light-dependent reactions** take place in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast. Here, chlorophyll absorbs light, water molecules are split, oxygen is released, and energy is captured in the form of molecules called ATP and NADPH.

The **light-independent reactions** (also called the Calvin cycle) happen in the stroma of the chloroplast. These use the ATP and NADPH from the first stage to convert CO₂ into glucose, which the plant uses for energy and growth.

Why it matters: photosynthesis is the foundation of most life on Earth. It produces the oxygen we breathe and forms the base of nearly every food chain, since plants are the primary producers that other organisms depend on.
